# SYNTH QUEST — Story Bible

*A JRPG for the monome norns. Four heroes. Four crystals. One world, sung.*

---

## 1. Cosmology & Lore

### The world is called HARMONIA.

Before there was a world, there was a held chord. The cosmologists of the great cities still call it the **First Tone** — a single sustained sound that contained, folded inside it like petals in a closed flower, every voice that would ever exist. In the beginning the First Tone simply *was*. There was no listener, because there was nothing else, and a sound with no listener and no silence around it is indistinguishable from eternity.

Then the First Tone *broke*. Not into pieces — into **partials**. The single sustained chord opened the way a beam of light opens through a prism, and from it spilled the ordered overtones that would become matter, weather, time, and song. This unfurling is called **The Articulation**, and it is the founding event of every theology in Harmonia. Reality, the priests sing, is not an object. It is a chord that has not yet finished resolving, and we are inside it.

The four enduring partials of the First Tone congealed, over uncountable ages, into the four **Crystals**.

### What the Crystals are.

A Crystal is not a gemstone. It is not a magical battery. A Crystal is a *living oscillator*: a piece of the First Tone that learned to remember its own shape and became, in remembering, a thing the world could touch. Each Crystal embodies one of the four pure ways a voice can be: pure tone, gentle harmonic body, hard structural pulse, shifting expressive contour.

- The **Crystal of Sine** sings, alone in the deep vaults of the Arx Sinaeum, a single perfect tone. It is the oldest. To stand near it is to feel mathematics become weather.
- The **Crystal of Triangle** breathes in the high cloister of Hemmel — a soft-cornered tone that holds whatever harmony is sung over it. The chorus that surrounds it is the prayers of seven generations of Clerics, sustained without pause.
- The **Crystal of Square** drives its blunt, periodic pulse out of an iron mountain in the Ostmark. It is the heartbeat the cities walk to. Smiths set their hammers to it. Soldiers march to it without knowing.
- The **Crystal of Wavetable** flickers on a moving stage somewhere on the road — it is the only Crystal that travels. It scans, slowly, through every voice the world has ever produced, and the tellers and bards who serve it follow wherever it goes.

The Crystals are alive in the only sense that matters: they listen. They harmonize. They can be wounded. They can dim.

### Why there are four.

The cosmologists teach that the First Tone, in opening, could only sustain *four* coherent partials before further division would have collapsed the world back into noise. The number is not symbolic. It is structural. Four is the most a chord can hold and still be a chord. (There were two further partials — the **Saw** and the **Noise** — that began to congeal but did not survive the Articulation. The Saw, restless and cutting, would not hold still long enough to coalesce; it dispersed into the outlaw winds of the world, where it survives in thieves' songs and in the cracked voices of those who live outside any tuning. The Noise, unpitched and unbound, was deemed too dangerous to be a Crystal at all — it was sealed by the first Conservatory in a place no map names, and the cult that still venerates it traffics in unmaking. They are not in the foreground of this age. They wait.)

### Why the world is dying.

When a Crystal dims, its partial drops out of the world. Not gradually — *categorically*. A dimmed Crystal of Triangle would not mean softer music; it would mean that the *body* of every sound — the warm middle of every voice — would simply cease. Bread would still be bread, but it would lose its taste of bread. People would still embrace, but the embrace would feel like a description of an embrace.

This has begun.

In the year the game opens, all four Crystals are flickering. The world has begun to sound *thin*. The wheat in the western fields ripens but no longer satisfies. The bells of the great cathedral in Reedhaven still ring, but they ring as if from very far away. Children born this season have voices a half-step under pitch, and no one can say why. The cosmologists, frightened, have begun to consult the oldest record they have — a tape, kept in the deepest vault of the Conservatory, on which the First Tone itself was once captured by a master long since dead. The tape, they whisper, is *spooling backward*.

Something is pulling the Articulation closed again.

## 2. The World — Harmonia and Its Regions

Harmonia is a single great continent ringed by four seas, with a fifth, smaller continent — the floating land of **Vox Aether** — drifting above it in the high stratosphere, reachable only by airship. The world is mapped on parchment as a node-graph; the player traverses it region to region, on foot at first and, after the midpoint, by skyship.

There are seven named regions. Five host plot beats. The other two are color and side-quest.

### Hemmelfeld — The Cloistered Valley (starting region, Floor 1)

A long green valley in the temperate north, terraced into vineyards and bell-orchards. The air of Hemmelfeld holds tones longer than air should — the local stone is full of small resonant cavities. Walk anywhere here and you will hear, faintly, the **Triangle Hymn**: a slow chorale sung in continuous round by the singers of the cloister at the valley's heart, sustained without break for over six hundred years. Every village has one or two singers in the rotation. The hymn is the valley's water table.

This is where the **Cleric** is born and where the game begins. Sonic character: gentle triangle pads, slight chorus, long hall reverb, distant bells. The **Crystal of Triangle** sleeps under the cloister.

### The Arx Sinaeum — The Glass Vault (Floor 2)

A vast subterranean library-fortress carved into a mountain on the eastern coast. The Arx houses the Conservatory — the world's school of cosmological music — and the **Crystal of Sine**. Its halls are long, narrow, and acoustically dead, lined in tuned glass; sounds passing through them are stripped of harmonics and emerge purified. The cosmologists who live here speak in *intervals* rather than full sentences — a fifth means *yes*, a tritone means *I disagree with my whole heart*. Their voices, after years of training, settle into something close to pure sine.

This is where the **Mage** comes from. Sonic character: dry sine, no reverb, a single ringing partial in any room. The Crystal of Sine is housed at the bottom of a vault so deep its echo never returns.

### The Ostmark — The Iron Marches (Floor 3)

A windswept industrial steppe where the rivers are diverted to drive immense water-hammers and the smithies never stop. The Ostmark is governed by guilds, and the guilds keep time by the **Crystal of Square**, housed in the central forge-temple of **Borvald** under a ceiling of suspended anvils. The Crystal's pulse is the metronome the entire region marches to: hammers fall on its downbeat, mills release on its upbeat, the guard changes on its third sixteenth.

The **Warrior** is from the Ostmark. Sonic character: square waves, sub-bass thuds on the bar, AR-fast hits, crisp tape-saturated low end. Soldiers here sing nothing — they *march*.

### The Reedmarsh — The Garden of Voices (mid-game, optional Crystal of Wavetable home)

A sprawling humid wetland in the south where every reed grows hollow and every tree is a resonator. The wind through the Reedmarsh is permanently tonal — the marsh sustains a stack of slowly modulating reed-organ chords, audible from two days' walk away. The traveling **Crystal of Wavetable** returns here often to rest, in a barge that drifts through the marsh on a slow rotation no one understands. The **Bard** is from this place.

Reedmarsh is also home to every traveler, recorder, and courtesan in Harmonia, because it is the only region where *recording* is legal. A small order of lacquer-pressers maintains the world's archives here under careful seal. Recording, elsewhere, is taboo — a recorded voice cannot move, and a voice that cannot move is a kind of small death.

Sonic character: low-passed detuned saws, a polyphonic reed-organ pad, the constant flutter of the marsh's own air column wowing itself.

### Glassmouth — The Coast of Grains (mid-game, color region; airship gateway)

A long curve of coast on the western sea where the ocean has been *sung into a single sustained granular pad*. Tiny windowed snippets of every wave that has ever broken here are caught in the salt air, looping at different rates — to walk the coast is to hear the whole history of the sea at once. Time feels strange in Glassmouth; people hear themselves speaking from years ago, and from years from now. The town of **Quay** is the world's busiest port and the only place in Harmonia where you can charter an **airship** — gas-bag vessels driven by tuned propeller-organs whose pitch determines their altitude. The higher the chord, the higher the ship.

This is the airship-unlocking region. Sonic character: a wide granular pad, long grain length, slow position drift, drenched in master tape.

### Vox Aether — The Floating Continent (Floor 4)

A small drifting landmass in the high stratosphere, reachable only by airship. Vox Aether is where the world's **Echoes** — the great summons — were originally enshrined, in seven old chapels around a central observatory. The bedrock is a vitrified resonator, half-transparent; you can see the slow turn of the world below your feet. The continent is held aloft by sustained chords sung by the four **Aeolian Choirs**, and one of the choirs has just *dropped out*. The continent is tilting.

This is where the party collects most of their Echoes and where the midpoint reveal happens. Sonic character: high airy pads, long delay, slow tape-flutter, the master tape in its highest harmonic register.

### The Coda — The Final Conservatory (Floor 5)

A vast, vertical, half-real cathedral rising through clouds at the world's center, accessible only after all four Crystals are restored. The Coda houses the master recording of the First Tone — and that tape is currently spooling backward. Each floor of the Coda is a higher partial made architecture: lower halls hum the fundamental, upper galleries shimmer in the eighth harmonic, the apex holds a single perfectly-tuned chord and the woman conducting it. To climb the Coda is to climb up *into the First Tone itself*, layer by layer.

Sonic character: every other region's drone, bussed together, slightly compressed, gradually slowing as the party ascends. The final dungeon is a piece of music.

### Outlying — The Splinterlands (color region, Saw rumor)

Ungoverned scrubland west of the Ostmark where the cutting wind is supposed to be the lost Saw partial, still loose. Outlaws and cracked balladeers live here. Side-quest only. The Saw cult is the seed for v2.

## 3. The Party — Four Chosen Heroes

### The Mage — ALDINE FARO of the Arx Sinaeum

Aldine is thirty-one. She is the youngest scholar ever to hold the chair of **Resonant History** at the Conservatory in the Arx Sinaeum, a position usually given to people in their seventies. She is small, dry-handed, and wears the iron-rimmed spectacles of her order. When she speaks she does so quietly, which the people who have not learned to listen mistake for shyness.

She is the **Sine** because her family were bell-tuners in a hamlet at the foot of the Arx, and she grew up with her ear pressed to bronze. She heard, by the age of six, partials that the academy's instruments could not measure. By twelve she could tune a cathedral bell from across a valley. By fifteen the Conservatory had taken her in. Her magic is the discipline of those years made articulate: a single pure tone, no harmonics, modulated through 2-op FM into bell-shaped strikes that ring out and decay.

Her flaw is that she believes precision and goodness are the same thing — that a correctly-tuned world is by definition a good world, and that grief is, on some unspeakable level, a tuning error. Her yearning is to one day hear the First Tone in full and not look away. Her secret is that her hometown — the bell-tuner village at the Arx's foot — was the **first place the master tape's reversal touched**. The bells there have gone hollow. Aldine returned home on holiday last winter and found her parents alive but *thinned*, as if rendered at half fidelity. She has not told the party. She suspects what she cannot say aloud: that the world's curative force, the very thing they are setting out to restore, may be the thing that thinned them.

Her arc is the longest of the four. She begins believing the Crystals are holy artifacts to be defended. She ends understanding that they are *living singers* who can be loved, and that love is not a tuning operation.

### The Cleric — BREN OYE of Hemmelfeld

Bren is nineteen. He is the **hymn-keeper** of the cloister at Hemmelfeld — a hereditary post he inherited, in a sense, by being born into the family that has held it for six generations, and in another sense by being the only one of his cohort who could carry the third part of the round without straining. He has the soft hands of a chorister and the worried eyes of someone who is responsible, every day at dusk, for not letting a six-hundred-year-old hymn fall silent.

He is the **Triangle** because he was raised inside the Hymn. Before he could speak he could hold a triangle-wave drone in tune, because everyone around him was holding one. His combat voice is gentle and chorused because the round itself is sung by twenty people slightly out of phase; his envelope is a pad because hymns are long. When he heals, he is *literally returning a voice to its true pitch*. When he hurts, he is asking the world to remember what it used to sound like.

His flaw is that he is too gentle — he forgives faster than he understands, and so he sometimes forgives the wrong thing. His yearning is to hear his mother's voice once more; she dropped out of the round two years ago, of natural causes, and he is not sure she went into the rest the Hymn promises or into something else. His secret is that he keeps a small, illegal **wax cylinder** of her voice, hidden in his bedroll. (Recording, in Hemmelfeld, is forbidden — to record a voice is to refuse to let it move on.) He plays it sometimes at night when the others are asleep. He knows he should not.

His arc is the cylinder. By the end of the game he will have to play it once, in front of someone, and let it go.

### The Warrior — GAUL STEN of Borvald

Gaul is forty-four and built like a load-bearing wall. He is a **Forge-Captain** of the Ostmark — a soldier-smith, the rank that exists because the Ostmark cannot tell its smiths from its soldiers and stopped trying centuries ago. He served in the long border-skirmishes against the Splinterlands raiders, came home with one bad shoulder and one good marriage, lost both within five years, and has been on the road as a private guard since. He does not say much. He is not stupid; he is *frugal* with words, the way a percussionist is frugal with strikes.

He is the **Square** because he grew up next to a forge. The pulse of a hammer on hot metal is a square wave. Gaul's combat voice is square plus sub-oscillator because his body produces fundamentals; his AR envelope is sharp because hammer-strikes are sharp. The Crystal of Square in Borvald is, in a real sense, the only god he has ever knelt to, although he would not put it that way.

His flaw is that he believes problems exist to be hit, and when one cannot be hit, he becomes useless to himself. His yearning is to find his daughter, **Mire**, who left home seven years ago to join a traveling theater troupe and has not written in three. His secret is that the Bard who joins the party in Hemmelfeld, **Lir**, was in Mire's troupe — was, briefly, very close to her — and knows what happened to her. Lir has not told him.

His arc is learning the rest. To hold a strike. To be a downbeat that does not always have to land. The first time, in the late game, that Gaul *does not swing*, the world will tilt around him.

### The Bard — LIR ANVI, of the Reedmarsh

Lir is twenty-six. They were born in a barge-troupe on the Reedmarsh's slowest river and have not had a fixed home since they were eight, when the troupe passed through Borvald and an aunt in Hemmelfeld and the long road and Quay and back. They use any pronouns the listener prefers and answer to all of them — half affectation, half truth. They carry a self-built wavetable instrument made of a found resonator and a length of tuned wire, and they can play anything they have heard once.

They are the **Wavetable** because their voice has had to be many things. They have played villains and lovers and animals and weather. Their envelope is expressive because their job has always been to react, second by second, to an audience. Their MS20-style filter is the resonant peak the Bards' Guild trains into every voice — a particular cry, between a third and a fourth above the fundamental, that the guild calls *the listening pitch*. To hear it is to feel the singer is paying attention to *you*.

Their flaw is that they lie reflexively, even when the truth would serve them. Their yearning is to write a song that is **true** — not entertaining, not moving, true. Their secret is that they were with **Mire Sten**, Gaul's daughter, when she stopped sounding, in a small Ostmark town the Empress's choristers passed through last winter. They have been carrying Mire's last song in their head, unsung, since. They do not yet know how to give it to her father.

Their arc is learning that the truest song they will ever write is the one they have to give away.

## 4. Main Plot Arc — Five Floors, Five Acts

### Floor 1 — Hemmelfeld. *The Hymn Falters.* (Inciting event)

The game opens at dusk in the cloister of Hemmelfeld. The Triangle Hymn — six hundred years unbroken — *falters*. A voice in the round drops out, then another, then four. The Cleric, **Bren**, runs to the cloister chapel and finds the **Crystal of Triangle dim** for the first time in living memory. Within the hour a traveling **Mage** from the Arx, Aldine, arrives by post-coach with a sealed letter from the Conservatory: every Crystal in Harmonia is failing. A road-guard in the village square, **Gaul**, volunteers his sword. A Bard who happened in last night with stories of stranger things, **Lir**, volunteers their tongue. The four are dispatched together, by the Hymn-Mother of Hemmelfeld, with the cloister's ceremonial relics and a single instruction: *find the cause, and do not stop until the Hymn is whole again.*

The Floor 1 climax is the **Trial of Triangle**: a sealed chapel beneath the cloister where the dimming originated. The boss is a *thinned* echo of the Hymn itself — the Crystal of Triangle, in pain, lashing out — and the win condition is to sing it back into round. With the first Crystal restored, the party emerges to news that **the master tape at the Coda is spooling backward**. They set out for the Arx Sinaeum.

### Floor 2 — The Arx Sinaeum. *The Conservatory Confesses.* (First crystal restored, scope revealed)

The Arx is in mourning. The provost is dead — peacefully, but unannouncedly, in a way the Conservatory knows how to recognize. He has been **resolved**, lifted out of the world by a force the Arx's oldest texts describe but do not name aloud. Aldine returns home for the first time in two years and hears at last what she has been refusing to hear: the agency behind the dimming has a name. **Empress Una Vesp** of the broken Empire of Vesperion. Once, before she was Empress, she was a student of this very Arx. The provost was her teacher. The texts she now uses to dim the Crystals were stolen, the night she left, from the room the party is standing in.

The Floor 2 climax is the **Trial of Sine** at the bottom of the deepest vault. The boss is the provost's last composition, animated and unfinished — it keeps trying to *resolve itself onto the party*. With Sine restored, Aldine receives, in the provost's hand, a sealed letter she was meant to read seven years ago. She does not yet open it.

### Floor 3 — The Ostmark. *The Empress Strikes.* (Midpoint reveal, raised stakes)

The party crosses west to the Ostmark to find the **Crystal of Square** under siege — not by monsters but by **Imperial Choristers**, Una's shock-troops, whose voices are sustained perfect-fifth chords held without breath. The Choristers do not raid. They *resolve*. They walk into a town singing one held chord, and the town stops moving. Borvald is half-resolved already; whole quarters of it stand frozen mid-strike, hammers raised, faces serene.

Gaul searches for word of his daughter. He does not find her. He finds, instead, **Lir breaking finally**, in the back room of a half-resolved tavern, telling him at last what happened to Mire. This is the heart of Act III. Gaul does not swing. Gaul does not hit anything. Gaul sits down on the tavern floor and does not move for a long time. Lir sings him Mire's last song. It is the first song they have ever sung that is true.

The Floor 3 climax is the **Trial of Square** in the Anvil-Temple. The boss is **Maestro Karran** — a former Forge-Captain, a man Gaul served under twenty years ago. Karran does not believe in the Empress; he believes in not feeling what he felt when his unit was destroyed in the border-war. Resolution is the only relief he has known. He fights Gaul to a standstill and is killed. Gaul feels no relief.

### Floor 4 — Vox Aether. *The Choir Drops Out.* (Tragedy / loss)

The party charters an airship from Quay and ascends to **Vox Aether**, the floating continent, to seek the wandering **Crystal of Wavetable** and the **Echoes** that wait there. The continent is tilting. One of the four Aeolian Choirs that hold it aloft has been resolved.

Lir, on Vox Aether, finds the Crystal of Wavetable in its barge — and finds, also, **Bren-Vesp**, the Empress's younger brother, who has defected. Bren-Vesp is the v1 Conductor's-defector character recast: a gentle man, too thoughtful to hold the line his sister has held. He travels with the party briefly and explains the Empress's true theology: she is not a tyrant. She is *grieving*. She lost someone, and she has decided that the only way to honor it is to make sure no one ever loses anything again.

The Floor 4 climax is the **Trial of Wavetable**: a moving stage on a mesa above the clouds. The boss is **Choirmaster Sela Vesp**, Bren-Vesp's wife and Una's chief Chorister. The fight is held inside her own resolution cadence, which grows louder over four bars and threatens to pull every party member out of the mix. She wins, in a sense. She kills Bren-Vesp in front of the party — gently, lovingly, *resolving him*, telling him she loves him as the chord closes around him. The party recovers. The Crystal of Wavetable is restored. Lir sings the elegy. The continent stops tilting, but only barely.

This is the worst thing that happens in the game. Then they fly to the Coda.

### Floor 5 — The Coda. *The First Tone.* (Climax)

The four Crystals are restored. The master tape is still spooling backward. The party ascends the Coda — a vertical dungeon that is the First Tone made architecture — toward the apex, where the Empress holds the tape against the world with her own voice.

At the apex, Una Vesp is alone. She is not a monster. She is a small woman at a console, weeping, holding a chord. Aldine opens the provost's letter and reads it aloud: it is about Aria Vesp, Una's wife, who died of a wasting illness in this Conservatory thirty years ago, and whose last recorded voice is the only thing on the master tape's other channel. Una has been spooling backward to find Aria's voice and *hold it forever*, so that no one — no one — ever has to lose what she lost.

The final boss is a **duet**. The Empress holds her chord; the party plays *around* her. The win condition is sixteen bars of sustained variation — rhythm, modulation, timbre, harmony — without any voice flatlining. If the party plays well enough, the Score itself convinces the Empress that resolution is one cadence among many, and that the people we have lost remain in our voices, not in the tape. She lets the chord open. The tape spins forward. Aria's voice is heard, briefly, by everyone at the apex. Then it ends, the way it ended thirty years ago. Una weeps. The Crystals brighten. The world keeps playing.

The party walks down the Coda in the morning. Hemmelfeld's Hymn, in the distance, has all its voices.

## 5. The Villain & Antagonists

### Empress UNA VESP — The Held Chord

Una Vesp was, before she was Empress, the most gifted student the Arx Sinaeum had ever seen. She was a composer of *long-form pieces* — works that took hours, sometimes days, to perform, and that resolved at the end into single sustained chords. She was famous, in Reedhaven and the Arx, for the moment of resolution: she could write toward a final note that felt, when it arrived, *inevitable*.

She fell in love, at the Conservatory, with a singer — **Aria Vesp**, a chorister of pure devotional Triangle — and they married in their early twenties. Aria fell ill in their twenty-eighth year of life. The illness was a **wasting** — Aria's voice thinned, over six years, until it could not be heard from across the room, and then could not be heard from across the bed, and then could not be heard at all. Una sat with her through every diminuendo. By the end, Una had come to believe that the cruelty of the world is *change* — that everything beautiful in Harmonia is beautiful only because it is being taken away. She decided to fix this.

She left the Arx the night Aria died, taking with her certain texts, and moved east into the broken kingdom of Vesperion, where her family had old claim. Within ten years she had an empire. Within twenty she had perfected the **resolution cadence** — the technique by which a sufficiently beautiful chord can convince a piece of the world to stop moving and hold itself, lovingly, in stasis forever. Her capital, **Coda Vesperion**, is the seed-city of what the players will encounter as the great spire — the master Conservatory itself, which she has reoccupied.

Her sonic signature is a single, perfectly-tuned, perfectly-stable held chord. No vibrato, no envelope, no decay. When she acts on the world, the world stops moving.

She is not evil. She is right about half of it. The world *does* take everything away. The party's task is not to prove her wrong — it is to argue that taking away is not the only thing the world does, and that the alternative she offers is a love that has stopped being able to listen.

### The Three Lieutenants

**Maestro KARRAN of the Anvil** — Una's eastern hand. Former Forge-Captain of the Ostmark, who survived a border-war ambush in which his entire unit was killed and has not slept a full night since. His sonic signature is a dense ostinato of square pulses on the bar, locked, unmoving, with a held perfect-fifth pad on top — the rhythm of marching through grief that does not stop. He tells himself he serves Una for the Empire. He serves her because resolution is the only quiet his head has known in twenty years. He fights Gaul in the Anvil-Temple. He is killed.

**Choirmaster SELA VESP of the Heights** — Una's most beloved Chorister, wife of Una's brother Bren-Vesp. Sela is a true believer in the kindest possible way: she resolves villages because she cannot bear to imagine those villagers grieving each other later. Her sonic signature is a five-voice sustained sine-pad chord, lush and final-sounding, that grows louder over four bars. She kills her own husband when he defects, gently. She is killed by the party on Vox Aether. This is the worst death in the game.

**The Apprentice ORIN** — A first-year student at the Arx, Aldine's prize pupil, who was found by the Empire after the Conservatory broke and who has been quietly studying resolution under Una for a year. The party meets Orin three times, in three regions, growing colder each time. Orin is the one who *survives*. Orin's arc is the question of whether grief learned young can ever be unlearned. The game does not answer.

### The Sympathetic Antagonist — BREN-VESP, *the brother who couldn't*

Una's younger brother, named for the soft-cornered Triangle their mother loved. Bren-Vesp joined the Empire after Aria's death because he loved his sister and because he, too, was grieving — Aria had been his sister-in-law for half his life. He served in the Imperial Choristers for seven years. He resolved villages. He believed, at first, that what he was doing was a kind of mercy.

He defected during a resolution in a small Ostmark town — he heard a child's voice in the chord and could not hold it. He has been on the road since, a fugitive from his own household, trying and failing to bring himself to *say to Una out loud what his refusal means*. He helps the party not because he wants Una stopped but because he wants her **heard** — by people who will love her and disagree with her at the same time, which is what he has been trying to do alone, and failing at, for two years.

He is killed, in Floor 4, by his own wife. Gently. She tells him she still loves him. She means it. He dies looking at her.

He is the FF6-Cyan / FF4-Kain register: the antagonist who turns out to be the most human person in the room.

## 6. Echoes — The Summons of Harmonia

The summons of Harmonia are called **Echoes**. Each Echo is a piece of legendary audio gear or technique, personified into a mythic being long ago, when the great gear-makers of the world walked it. To summon an Echo in combat is to invoke the technique itself — to wrap the party's voices, briefly, in the Echo's signature processing.

There are seven major Echoes and one rumored.

### TELEPHA — The Tape Echo of the Old Lighthouse

A long-haired, salt-bleached figure in the lantern-room of an abandoned lighthouse on Glassmouth's southern point. Telepha is the personified **tape-delay machines** of the early empires — repeating any voice she hears at slowly decaying intervals, with the sweet wow-and-flutter that tape gives. Summoned, she repeats every party attack twice more at a settable feedback. Myth: she was the first listener, the spirit who taught the world to *remember sound*.

### VOXIRA — The Vocoder of the Sealed Cathedral

A high crystalline figure with a face of moving mouths. The personified **vocoder** — the technique by which one voice borrows the formants of another. Summoned, she modulates the party's voices through the boss's, briefly turning the boss's own resonance into a weapon against it. Myth: a chorister who, in mourning, wished to speak with her dead lover's mouth — and was granted the power to speak with anyone's.

### PHASORIN — The Analog Phaser of the Rolling Hills

A low rolling figure, half-cloud, half-river. The personified **analog phaser** — the swirling notch-comb that gives a voice motion without changing its pitch. Summoned, he sets every enemy's voice into slow phase modulation, smearing their attack timing across the bar. Myth: a wanderer who could never stand still, given a body that is always slightly behind itself.

### CHORALIS — The Stereo Chorus of the Twin Pines

Twin sisters who finish each other's sentences slightly out of phase. The personified **stereo chorus** — the doubled, slightly-detuned, slightly-delayed voice that turns one singer into many. Summoned, they triple every party voice in stereo, granting wide harmonic body and sustain. Myth: a single child cried for so long by both parents that the world doubled her, so they could each hold one.

### RINGOLD — The Ring Modulator of the Cracked Tower

A bell-shaped figure with two clappers that strike at incommensurable intervals. The personified **ring modulator** — the demon-technique by which two pure tones are multiplied to produce strange inharmonic sums and differences. Summoned, he ring-modulates the boss's voice with the party's, producing chaotic sideband attacks. Myth: Ringold is feared. He was once two lovers; their voices were ring-modded together by a jealous mage and they have spoken since only in inharmonic clangor. They are both still in there.

### GRANULA — The Cloud of the Coast

A drifting figure made of thousands of tiny windowed bodies. The personified **granular cloud** — a voice shattered into grains and reassembled into a pad. Summoned, she grains the party's voices into a sustaining cloud, granting damage-over-time pad coverage. Myth: she is the Glassmouth coast itself, every wave that ever broke there given a single dreaming face.

### SIDRA — The Sidechain of the Master Bus

A small busy figure who breathes in time with whatever room she is in. The personified **sidechain compressor** — one voice ducking every other in time. Summoned, she pumps the boss's voice against the party's strikes, silencing it on the downbeat. Myth: the first conductor to step out of her own performance to listen to it, rewarded by the world with the gift of every voice yielding to hers, just a little.

### *(Rumored)* THE FEEDBACK — *the eighth, sealed*

Whispered of in the deepest Reedmarsh archives. A self-amplifying entity that, once summoned, cannot be banished. The party finds references to it. They do not summon it. v2.

## 7. Towns and Their People

### HEMMELFELD-CLOISTER (Hemmelfeld)

A stone village around a domed cloister, bell-orchards in concentric rings. Sonic character: a low triangle drone bleeding from the cloister, soft chorus, long hall reverb. At dusk the whole village joins the round.

- **Hymn-Mother MAREN OYE** (Bren's older sister) — soft low triangle, dry. *"The Hymn does not wait for any one of us. That is the kindness of it."* Dispatches the party; will not let Bren leave with the cylinder unless he admits he has it.
- **OLD FERRO** — the oldest singer in the round, voice almost lost in the tape's hiss. *"I remember when the Hymn was sung in three parts. It was thinner then. We were braver."* Knows the lost second verse.
- **THE ROUND-MASTER** (title only; the role costs the holder her name) — the sustained low triangle root of the round. Will teach Bren the lost middle register if he plays his mother's cylinder for her once.

### ARX SINAEUM (the Arx Sinaeum)

The Conservatory-fortress. Glass-lined halls, dim libraries. Sonic character: dry sine, no reverb, single ringing partials. The cosmologists *interval-greet* each other in the corridors — a fifth, a sixth, a hesitant tritone.

- **PROVOST ANWEN** — Aldine's old mentor. (Dies in Floor 2.) Cracked sine with one rough partial. Leaves the sealed letter that turns the plot.
- **KELL THE CYLINDER-MAKER** — keeps the forbidden archive. Dry granular voice with a constant low click. *"Every voice I press onto wax is one that won't move on without me. I know what I do."* Holds a sealed cylinder of the young Una Vesp, singing.
- **APPRENTICE ORIN** — Aldine's prize pupil. Pure sine, no warmth. *"You taught me to keep my voice clean, professor. I have."* Vanishes from the Arx after Floor 2.

### BORVALD-ON-THE-ANVIL (the Ostmark)

A grid-laid forge-city around a temple of suspended anvils. Sonic character: square pulses on the bar, sub-bass thuds, crisp tape-saturated low end. Hammers everywhere, until the resolution.

- **GUILDMASTER HERR-VOL** — square-and-sub voice, AR-fast. *"The Crystal does not pause. Neither do we."* Grants access to the Anvil-Temple if Gaul fights three rounds in the city square.
- **THE WIDOW OF KARRAN** — a soft triangle in the middle of all the squares. *"He used to come home and not speak for hours. I thought I had failed him. I had not."* The only person in Borvald who knows what Karran's grief actually is.
- **MIRE'S OLD LANDLADY** — square voice, slightly out of tune. *"There was a girl. She paid in coin and song. She left in the autumn."* The breadcrumb that pulls Gaul toward the tavern scene with Lir.

### REEDFORD-IN-THE-MARSH (Reedmarsh)

A barge-village on stilts. Sonic character: low-passed detuned saws, reed-organ pad, marsh-flutter. Lacquer-pressers in low huts.

- **HARBORMASTER ODE** — wide pad voice with grains of other voices riding it. Charters airships, knows every troupe.
- **THE LACQUER-PRESSER YENI** — soft sine with gentle wow. *"Every cylinder is a small cage. I make beautiful cages."* Pressed the cylinder of Bren's mother; recognizes him on sight.
- **CRINK** — a Hummel (see below). A small cheerful sine with a soft AR envelope. *"Sing? Sing!"* Follows the party once fed.

### QUAY-ON-GLASSMOUTH (Glassmouth)

A port-town on the granular sea. Seashell buildings. Citizens have learned not to listen too closely. Sonic character: wide granular pad, long delay, ghostly conversation in the salt wind.

- **SKY-CAPTAIN ENVELLE** — wavetable voice, theatrical. *"The fleet flies on chords. Get the chord wrong, the ship comes down. Pay extra for tenor."* The airship.
- **THE SINGING CHILD** — clean wavetable scan, layered against the granular pad. In danger of being granulated into the sea. Lir takes a particular interest.
- **OLD GULL** — detuned saw, frequently out of tune with himself. *"Used to be the sea was just water. Now it remembers."* Remembers the coast pre-grain.

### The HUMMELS — *the recurring helpful creature*

Small round furry creatures the size of an apple, shaped like a stylized speaker cone, who live in the Reedmarsh and migrate alongside Bards. A Hummel *is* a small oscillator: each one has a single fundamental, and you can tune a chorus by singing at them in turn. They follow Bards because Bards scan — a wavetable voice contains every Hummel's note somewhere. Feed one and it sits on your shoulder for the rest of the run; feed it well and it sings a single perfect note in the next boss fight, on the bar, granting a rare modulation slot. The recurring Hummel is **CRINK**, slightly out of tune, who attaches in Reedford. Crink also sings the closing tone of the final cutscene.

(The mounts of Harmonia are **Metronomes**: walking donkey-sized clockworks with arms that swing in tempo. Riding one locks you, briefly, into its tempo. They are stubborn; they will not change for the rider. The rider must change for them.)

## 8. Enemies and Bosses

### Enemies (twelve)

1. **Chime-Hounds** *(Hemmelfeld)* — pure sine impulses with bell envelopes. Fire on downbeats of every other bar. Tactic: read the metronome. *Tutorial.*
2. **Round-Wraiths** *(Hemmelfeld)* — triangle voices that *join the party's round*, harmonizing with whatever rhythm the party falls into. Fire on offbeats only after the party has not varied for four bars. Tactic: keep the rhythm changing.
3. **Chord-Nuns** *(Arx Sinaeum)* — five-part sustained sine-pad chords, walking the dry corridors in slow procession. Fire as a chord arpeggio across the bar. Tactic: silencing one voice changes the chord; pick the one that resolves into something less threatening.
4. **Bowmouths** *(Outlying / Bellow)* — bowed metallic FM, microtonal, very slow attack. Fire on long sustained crescendos. Tactic: hit them in the attack ramp.
5. **Anvil-Drudges** *(Ostmark)* — square-and-sub punches on the downbeat, ostinato locked. Fire as a four-on-the-floor pattern. Tactic: their attack is loud but predictable; the player can time defense to the kick.
6. **Imperial Choristers** *(Ostmark, Vox Aether)* — humanoid, reluctant. Voice: a perfectly-held sine-pad chord, dry and motionless. Fire long sustained "resolution cadences" that grow louder over four bars; if completed, *resolve a party member out of the encounter for a turn*. Tactic: interrupt their cadence with rhythmic disruption — out-*move* them, not out-damage them.
7. **Reed-Choirs** *(Reedmarsh)* — five-voice low-passed saw stacks fighting as one entity, each voice on its own ATB clock. Fire as a chord progression across four bars. Tactic: silence the dissonant voice and let the chord settle gently.
8. **Cylinder-Wights** *(Reedmarsh secret)* — old recordings escaped from the archive, looping a fragment of a dead person's last song. Fire as ostinatos locked to the bar. Tactic: the only way to stop them permanently is to *let the loop finish*, which takes a full encounter.
9. **Granulines** *(Glassmouth)* — granular pads that loop fragments of the party's own previous attacks back at them. Fire on whatever rhythm the party last used. Tactic: novelty. They have no defense against a move they have never heard.
10. **Aeolian Wraiths** *(Vox Aether)* — wisp-bodied entities that are the *resolved* members of the dropped Aeolian Choir. Voice: a single sustained note held until they touch a party member, at which point they release the note and dissolve. Tactic: they are not killed. They are sung *back*.
11. **Hush-Crickets** *(near the Coda)* — sharp tape-hiss bursts, no pitched component. Fire in fast unpredictable stutters, three-against-four. Tactic: deal with them first; their bursts mask other tells.
12. **Hush-Holes** *(Coda, environmental)* — patches of true silence on the field. Walking through one removes a party member from the mix entirely for two bars. Tactic: route around, or sing them shut by sustaining a note across the gap.

### Bosses (five — one per crystal trial, plus the final)

1. **THE FALTERING HYMN** *(Hemmelfeld, Crystal of Triangle).* The Crystal in pain. A massive five-voice triangle-wave round, each voice sustained, the whole structure *thinning* in real time as the fight goes on. Boss music: a hymn that loses voices as it is sung. Win condition: hold every part of the round in tune for four full bars of unbroken sustain — sing the Crystal *back into round*, do not damage it. Bittersweet. The first lesson: not every fight is to the death.
2. **THE PROVOST'S LAST COMPOSITION** *(Arx Sinaeum, Crystal of Sine).* The provost's unfinished final piece, animated. A 2-op FM bell-strike entity that keeps trying to *resolve itself onto the party* — every successful cadence pulls a party member's voice toward stasis. Boss music: a piece that builds toward an inevitable cadence, four bars at a time. Mechanic: interrupt every fourth cadence with a non-resolving interval. Aldine, narratively, is the one who finishes it — by *transposing the whole piece up a fifth* on the last beat, refusing the resolution.
3. **MAESTRO KARRAN OF THE ANVIL** *(Ostmark, Crystal of Square).* A square-and-sub fighter on a relentless four-on-the-floor ostinato. Boss music: a march. Mechanic: Karran's attack pattern is locked to the bar and *unbreakable*; the only way to win is for one party member (Gaul) to *miss a beat on purpose* — to break the ostinato by refusing to strike on the downbeat. Karran cannot fight what is not on the grid. The first time Gaul does not swing.
4. **CHOIRMASTER SELA VESP OF THE HEIGHTS** *(Vox Aether, Crystal of Wavetable).* A five-voice sustained sine-pad chord with a wavetable scan above it, voiced by the woman who has just killed her own husband. Boss music: a love song so beautiful it kills. Mechanic: every time a party member's HP drops below half, her chord resolves a step closer to the party's key, making her cadences harder to interrupt. The party has to fight *out of tune with her*, deliberately. Lir, narratively, is the one who lands the final voice — by singing Bren-Vesp's true name, which Sela has not heard spoken in two years.
5. **EMPRESS UNA VESP — *the held chord*** *(Coda, final).* Not a kill. A duet. Una holds a single perfectly-tuned sustained chord at the apex of the world. Resolution radiates from her in slow waves, gradually flattening every party member's envelope, slowing their ATB, low-passing their voices. Boss music: the master tape, played forward and backward simultaneously. Mechanic: maintain enough rhythmic, harmonic, and timbral *variation* across all four party members for sixteen bars without any voice flatlining. The boss does not die. She **moves**. Her chord becomes a passing tone. The Score keeps playing.

## 9. Tone — Three Voice Samples

### Opening Narration

> Long before the cities, before the bell-orchards of Hemmelfeld and the iron mountains of the Ostmark, there was a single sustained sound that no one was alive to hear. It was vast, and it was patient, and it contained — folded inside it, the way a struck bell contains its overtones — every voice that would ever exist. And then, for reasons no cosmologist has ever satisfactorily explained, the First Tone *opened*. It unfurled into four enduring partials, and from those partials grew the Crystals that hold the world together: the bright Sine, the gentle Triangle, the steady Square, and the shifting Wavetable. The world is the song that began that day, and it is still being sung, and we are inside the singing of it.
>
> Tonight, in a small valley called Hemmelfeld, a six-hundred-year-old hymn falters at dusk. A voice in the round drops out, and then another, and the singers who remain do not yet understand what they are hearing. Far to the east, an Empress holds a chord. She is not finished holding it. The world is about to need four voices, and four voices are about to be needed. The Hymn waits.

### NPC Dialogue — Old Ferro on the porch of the round-house, Hemmelfeld

> Old Ferro is sitting on the porch of the round-house, both hands flat on his knees, eyes closed. The cloister's drone hangs in the air around him — a soft triangle pad, half a chord above the village's own sung hum, with the whole village's chorus settling on top like a long warm coat. His own voice, when it comes, is so quiet you can hear the master tape's hiss around it.
>
> "You want to know the second verse."
>
> "I do."
>
> "Nobody's sung the second verse since the year my father stopped. My father stopped because his sister stopped, and his sister stopped because her son stopped, and the second verse is for stopping." He opens one eye and looks at Bren with what is, in Hemmelfeld, the closest thing to a smile. "You'll need it before this is over. Sit a while. Hum the first verse with me. If we hum it through to the gap and then keep going past it, sometimes the second verse remembers itself for me. Sometimes."

### Boss Encounter Intro — Choirmaster Sela Vesp, Vox Aether

> The mesa is a wide stone disk above the cloud-line, ringed by seven old chapels, none of them whole. Choirmaster Sela Vesp is standing alone at the centre. Her husband is on the ground at her feet, very still. The wind on Vox Aether is an A-flat held by a choir that no longer has all its voices, and the A-flat is wavering. Sela does not look at the party as they cross the disk. She is humming. A single note, perfectly in tune with the missing voice in the wind.
>
> Then she looks up. Her face is wet. Her voice, when it comes, is the most beautiful thing the party has ever heard, and it is shaped exactly like the chord that has just ended a man.
>
> *"He was tired. I gave him rest. I would do that for any of you. Let me do that for any of you."*
>
> The boss music begins. It is a love song. It is going to try, very gently, very seriously, to kill them.
