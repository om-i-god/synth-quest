# SYNTH QUEST — Story Bible v0.1

*A small JRPG for the monome norns, in which playing the game is playing the instrument.*

---

## 1. Cosmology

### The world is called Aural.

Not "the world of Aural." Just Aural. The word is the place. To name it is to be inside it.

Aural is not a planet. It is a held chord. A long, slow, unfinished performance that began before anything and is still going on. Mountains, oceans, animals, weather, people — all of these are *partials* of that chord, the way a single struck bell contains a fundamental tone and a shimmering tower of overtones above it. When something exists, it is because it is *currently sounding*. When it stops sounding, it stops existing. There is no philosophical death in Aural. There is only the moment a voice drops out of the mix.

Reality, then, is a performance. And like any performance, it has structure: a meter, a key, a form. The cosmologists call this **the Score**, but they will tell you, if pressed, that the Score is not written down anywhere. It is not a thing being read. It is a thing being *remembered together* by everyone playing it. Aural exists because it is being agreed upon, beat by beat, by every voice in it.

### The First Note

Before Aural sounded, there was the **Hum** — a flat, unmodulated, infinite drone, neither loud nor soft, neither pitched nor unpitched. The Hum was not silence. Silence is the *absence* of sound; the Hum is sound that has never been articulated. A held breath that was never released into a word.

The First Note was the moment the Hum broke. The traditional story is that something — call it the First Player, though even children know that name is a placeholder — *bent* the Hum. Not loudly. Not violently. Just enough to introduce a difference: a pitch where there had been no pitch, a contour where there had been a flatline. That bend is still propagating. Aural is the wavefront of that first articulation, expanding outward through whatever it is that articulation expands through. Everything that has ever happened in Aural is a sub-articulation of that First Note: a grace note, a turn, a passing tone, a fermata.

This is why most spells, charms, and prayers in Aural are sung or played rather than spoken. Words are too narrow. To act on the world you have to *match* the world's frequency, and the world's frequency is musical.

### The Six Voices

Long after the First Note but long before recorded history, the Score braided itself into **six fundamental voices**, sometimes called the Sixfold or the Choir Beneath. Every living person in Aural is, when you listen carefully, harmonically *aligned* with one of the six. You don't pick. You hear yourself, eventually, and you know.

The four voices currently in the Score's foreground are:

- **The Sine** — pure, monastic, ancient. The bell rings of the world. Mages.
- **The Triangle** — gentle, sustained, devotional. The breath of the world. Clerics.
- **The Square** — blunt, periodic, structural. The pulse of the world. Warriors.
- **The Wavetable** — shifting, narrative, theatrical. The story of the world. Bards.

Two voices have largely fallen out of the active mix and exist mostly in old songs and rumors:

- **The Noise** — the unpitched residue, the breath between notes, what the world sounds like when it is between things. Said to be the voice of those who walk in negative space. Necromancers, by some accounts.
- **The Saw** — the cutting voice, the one that takes a piece of every other note as it goes by. Thieves, said with a wink in the trade-roads, but also: anyone who survives by making themselves slightly out of tune with everything around them.

The party in Synth Quest are *literal voices in the Score*. They are not characters who happen to make music. They are voices that happen to also be characters. When the Mage casts a spell, the spell *is* her voice. When the Warrior strikes, the strike *is* his voice. There is no separation, ever. Combat is performance, exploration is performance, dialogue is performance. The audience is the world, and also the world is the orchestra, and also you are listening.

### Tuning is morality

In Aural, the most fundamental ethical concept is **tuning**. To be in tune is not just to be sonically correct; it is to be *in right relationship with the rest of the Score*. A liar is detuned. A betrayer is detuned. A grieving person is detuned, but rightfully so — grief is a ratio that the Score itself contains, and refusing to grieve is its own kind of detuning. Healing in Aural is, almost without exception, a process of returning a voice to its true pitch. (This is what Clerics literally do, mechanically, with their pad-envelope hymns.)

### Silence

Silence in Aural is not peace. Silence is *unmaking*. A truly silent place is a place that has been argued out of the Score. There is one such place. It is large and growing. We will get to it.

## 2. The World / Overworld

The overworld is traversed as a node-graph map between runs. Six named regions, four of which are reachable in the v1 prototype's three-floor structure. Each region's drone is the literal sound of being there: walking through Pleach, you hear Pleach. The combat encounters layer on top of that floor drone.

### Pleach

The starting region. A river-valley of low, soft hills covered in something between moss and felt. Rain comes through about every twenty minutes, lightly, and the ground absorbs it without sound. The drone of Pleach is a single triangle wave through a long hall reverb with a slow chorus, slightly flattened by the master tape. Walking here feels like the first second after a hymn. This is the tutorial region: villages are close together, monsters are slow and meter-locked, the air itself is forgiving.

### The Bellow

A wide, dry basin where the wind moves in long sustained tones because the rocks are shaped like mouths. The Bellow is full of stone instruments left by people who came before, half-buried, half-tuned by erosion. The drone is bowed metallic FM — long, scraping, microtonal, with the master tape pulling the highs down into something brassy and cracked. Here, the player learns that the world is *played* by something other than them. The wind is its own instrument and it does not care.

### The Reedmarsh

A flooded forest where every tree is hollow and the wind through them produces a permanent, polyphonic reed-organ chord. The drone is a stack of detuned saws low-passed almost to mud, with a flutter that is the master tape head-bumping in real time. Reedmarsh is humid, slightly hostile, and full of small clearings where the chord briefly resolves and a town can exist. Magical research is done here because the air itself sustains tones longer than anywhere else.

### Glassmouth Coast

A long curve of shore where the sea has, over time, been sung into a single sustained granular pad. Grains of the ocean — *literally*, tiny windowed snippets of past wave-noise — are caught in the air, looping at different rates, so that walking the coast you hear the whole history of the sea simultaneously, granulated. The drone is a wide granular pad with long grain length, low density, slow position drift. This is the midgame region. Time feels strange here.

### The Hush

The dead zone. A growing region in the eastern interior where the Score has been lifted out. There is no drone in The Hush. The master tape still runs (the world cannot turn it off), so what you hear in The Hush is the warmth of the tape *itself*, with nothing on it: hiss, wow, flutter, the ghost of recordings the tape has held before. The Hush is the antagonist's footprint. It is silent the way a room is silent after someone you loved has just stopped speaking forever. This is the late-game region.

### The Coda (final-act region)

A vertical region: a tower of held chords stacked on each other like geological strata. The Coda is *the place where the Score is loudest* — the closest you can stand to the original First Note without becoming it. Reaching the Coda feels like climbing into a cathedral that is also a chord, where each story up is a higher partial. The drone here is not invented; it is the live mix of every other region's drone, bussed together, slightly compressed by the master tape, and the player is walking inside that mix. This is the final dungeon.

## 3. Main Plot Arc

### Premise

A figure who calls herself **The Conductor** has decided that the Score is wrong. Not evil — *wrong*. She is not a tyrant; she is a perfectionist. Her claim is that the First Note was a mistake — that the original bend in the Hum introduced *suffering*, because suffering is what happens when a voice has to keep changing in order to stay alive. She does not want to destroy Aural. She wants to *resolve* it: to gently, lovingly, and irreversibly bring every voice in the Score to a single shared pitch and hold it there forever. One eternal, perfectly-tuned, unmoving chord. No more change. No more loss. No more grief.

This is what The Hush is: not death, but **resolution**. Every place she has visited has been brought to its final cadence and held there. The hiss the player hears in The Hush is the residual love of the world for what used to be playing, before The Conductor smoothed it.

The party's task is not to kill her. It is to keep the Score *moving*.

### Acts

**Act I — The First Drop-Out (Floor 1, Pleach).** The story opens in a small Pleach village called **Hemmel**, where someone has stopped sounding. Not died — *stopped*. The villagers can't remember her name, because her name was a sequence of tones in the Score and those tones are no longer being held. The Mage, a researcher visiting Hemmel, recognizes the signature of what's happened: someone has been *resolved*. She rallies a Cleric (the village's hymn-keeper), a Warrior (a road-guard who happened to be there), and a Bard (a traveler who arrived the night before with a story about somewhere else this had happened). The four leave Hemmel to follow the Conductor's path.

**Act II — The Bowed Country (Floor 2, The Bellow / Reedmarsh).** The party crosses two regions and learns the scale of the problem. Whole villages have been hushed. They encounter the Conductor's lieutenants — her **Tuners** — out in the world, gently and politely escorting villages into resolution. It is genuinely difficult to fight people who believe they are doing kindness. Midpoint: the party meets **Vesp**, a former Tuner who has defected. He explains the Conductor's true theology: she is not a fanatic. She is grieving. He says, "She lost something, and she has decided that the only way to honor it is to make sure no one ever loses anything again." The party doesn't know whether to trust him.

**Act III — The Granular Sea (Glassmouth Coast).** On the coast, the party finds a **fragment of the original First Note** caught in the granular pad of the sea — a single grain that has been looping there since the world began. Touching it, the Mage hears what the world sounded like the moment it began bending out of the Hum, and understands, for the first time, why the Conductor wants what she wants: the First Note is, in fact, *painful*. It is the sound of a perfect held thing being broken so that things could happen. Things have happened ever since, and many of them have been bad. The Mage almost agrees with the Conductor. This is the dark night. Vesp is killed here, by a Tuner who used to be his wife, who is gentle about it.

**Act IV — Into The Hush (The Hush).** The party crosses into the dead zone. There is no floor drone. Combat in The Hush is *quieter* — the tape hiss is louder than the synthesis, and players have to fight in something close to acoustic silence. The party recovers, in The Hush, the *names* of the people who were resolved there. Names are returned to the Score by being sung. By the end of the act, the four of them have brought a small patch of The Hush back into pitch.

**Act V — The Coda.** The final ascent. The Conductor is at the top of the Coda, holding the Score in place at the moment of ultimate resolution. The final boss fight is not a fight to silence her — it is a fight to *play around her*. Mechanically: the boss holds a sustained chord and the party has to introduce enough movement, enough rhythmic variation, enough modulation, to convince the Score that *resolution is just one possible cadence among many*. The ending is bittersweet. The Conductor is not killed. She is *moved* — turned from a held note into a passing tone. She becomes a voice in the Score again, mourning what she lost, but mourning *forward*. The Hush stops growing. Aural keeps playing.

## 4. Party Member Backstories

### The Mage — Aldine Faro

Aldine is the daughter of a family of bell-tuners from a Pleach hamlet that no longer exists. As a child she could hear when bells were being tuned three valleys away, and she grew up assuming everyone could. She left home at fifteen to study at the academy in Reedmarsh, where they teach the geometry of the First Note. She is now thirty-one and holds a junior chair in **Resonant History**.

Aldine is the **Sine** because she has spent most of her life trying to be the smallest possible disturbance in a room. She talks softly, walks softly, takes up no space, and her magical voice is the consequence of that discipline: a single pure tone with no harmonics, modulating itself through 2-op FM into bell-shaped strikes. Her envelope is a bell because her family made bells.

Her flaw is that she believes precision and goodness are the same thing. Her yearning is to one day hear the First Note in full and not look away. Her secret is that her hometown was the first village the Conductor resolved, and that Aldine, returning from the academy, mistook the resulting silence for *peace* for almost a full day before she understood. She has not told the party that she has stood in The Hush before.

Her arc: learning that the Score's value is in its *movement*, not its *purity*, and that grief is not a bug.

### The Cleric — Bren Oye

Bren is the hymn-keeper of Hemmel, the village in Pleach where the game starts. He is nineteen. He inherited the role from his mother, who inherited it from her father, who inherited it from no one — he just started one day. The Oye family does not strictly own the role, but no one in Hemmel can remember a time it wasn't theirs.

Bren is the **Triangle** because the village hymn he was raised on is a triangle-wave round, sung at dusk by everyone in Hemmel for as long as anyone can remember. His voice is gentle and chorused because the round itself is sung by twenty people slightly out of phase. His envelope is a pad because hymns are long.

His flaw is that he is *too* gentle — he forgives faster than he understands, which means he sometimes forgives the wrong thing. His yearning is to hear his mother's voice again; she stopped sounding two years ago, of natural causes, and he is not sure she went into death or into The Hush. His secret is that he keeps a small, illegal recording of her on a wax cylinder, hidden in his bedroll. (Recordings, in Aural, are taboo: they hold a voice in place when the Score wants it to move. They are how The Conductor began her career.)

His arc: learning that the cylinder must, eventually, be played and let go of, and that holding on is its own kind of hush.

### The Warrior — Gaul Sten

Gaul is a road-guard from the eastern marches who happened to be passing through Hemmel the night the first villager went silent. He is forty-four and built like a load-bearing wall. He does not say much. He is not stupid; he is *frugal* with words, in the way that a percussionist is frugal with strikes.

Gaul is the **Square** because his father was a smith and Gaul grew up next to a forge. The pulse of a hammer on hot metal is, structurally, a square wave. Gaul's combat voice is square+sub because his body is large and produces fundamentals, and because a smith's strike contains its own sub-tone in the anvil. His AR envelope is sharp because hammer strikes are sharp.

His flaw is that he believes problems exist to be hit, even when they don't. His yearning is to find his daughter, who left home seven years ago to join a traveling theater troupe and has not written in three. His secret is that the bard who joined the party in Hemmel, **Lir**, knew his daughter — was, briefly, in the same troupe — and has not yet told him what happened to her.

His arc: learning to hold his strikes, to *rest*, to be a downbeat that does not always have to land.

### The Bard — Lir Anvi

Lir is twenty-six, a traveling performer with a wavetable instrument they built themselves out of a found resonator and a length of tuned wire. They use any pronouns the listener prefers and answer to all of them — this is part of the act, but also true. Lir grew up in a theater troupe and has not had a fixed home since they were eight.

Lir is the **Wavetable** because their voice has had to be many things. They have played villains and lovers and animals and weather. Their envelope is expressive because their job has always been to react, second by second, to an audience. Their MS20-style filter is the result of years of vocal training in the troupe's signature style, which involves resonant peaks at culturally meaningful frequencies.

Their flaw is that they lie reflexively, even when the truth would serve them better. Their yearning is to write a song that is *true* — not entertaining, not moving, *true*. Their secret is that they were with Gaul's daughter, **Mire**, when she stopped sounding, in a small Bellow town the Conductor passed through last winter. They have been carrying her last song in their head since, unsung, and they do not know how to give it to him.

Their arc: learning that the truest song they will ever write is the one they have to give away.

## 5. Antagonists

### The Conductor — Una Vesp

Yes, **Vesp**. The defected Tuner the party meets in Act II is Una's younger brother Bren-Vesp (no relation to Bren Oye; "Vesp" is a Reedmarsh family name), and he is the one mid-game antagonist who turns out to be more nuanced than expected — except in this case the twist is sideways: he's not the villain, he's an apostate from the villain's own household.

Una Vesp was, before any of this, a composer. Specifically, she was a composer of *long-form pieces* — works that took hours, sometimes days, to perform, and that resolved at the end into single sustained chords. She was famous, in Reedmarsh circles, for the moment of resolution: she could write toward a final note that felt, when it arrived, *inevitable*.

Then her wife, the singer **Aria Vesp**, died slowly of a wasting illness over six years. Aria's voice — a true triangle, devotional and pad-enveloped — thinned and thinned until it could not be heard from across the room, and then could not be heard from across the bed, and then could not be heard at all. Una sat with her through every diminuendo. By the end, Una had come to believe that the cruelty of the Score is *change* — that everything beautiful, in Aural, is beautiful only because it is being *taken away*. She decided to fix this.

Her sonic signature: a single, perfectly-tuned, perfectly-stable held chord. No vibrato, no envelope, no decay. When she acts on the world, the world stops moving. Her Tuners — her followers, mostly bereaved people she has gathered — produce *resolution cadences*, lush and final-sounding, that pull whatever they encounter toward stasis.

What she does to the world's sound: she does not silence it. She *holds* it. The Hush is not the absence of music; it is music *that has been resolved and is now sustaining forever, just below the threshold of audibility, with no further movement*. The hiss the player hears is the master tape's own voice, the only thing still moving in there.

Why she's compelling: she's right about half of it. The Score *does* take everything away. That is in fact what it does. The party's job is not to prove her wrong — it's to argue that *taking away* is not the only thing the Score does, and that the alternative she's offering is a kind of love that has stopped being able to listen.

### Bren-Vesp (mid-game, more nuanced than expected)

Una's younger brother. Joined the Tuners with her after Aria died because he loved his sister and was also grieving. Spent three years escorting villages into resolution before something in him refused. He defected during a resolution in The Bellow — he heard a child's voice in the chord and could not hold it.

Bren-Vesp is the nuance. He does not believe the Tuners are evil. He believes they are *correct about the diagnosis and wrong about the treatment*. He helps the party not because he wants Una stopped but because he wants her *heard* — by people who will love her and disagree with her at the same time, which is what he has been trying to do alone, and failing at, for two years.

He dies in Act III. Quietly. By his wife's hand. She is gentle about it. She tells him she still loves him as she resolves him, and she means it. This is the worst thing that happens in the game.

## 6. Towns and NPCs

The Town-as-Mixer concept is parked for v2 — for v1, towns are diegetic locations with shopkeepers, quest-givers, and rest points. But each town is built around a sonic premise.

### Hemmel (Pleach)

The starting town. Twenty-some triangle-wave families in a valley, sung together at dusk every day in a round that has been ongoing for six generations. The whole town is, in effect, a chorus pad. Quest hook: someone in the round has dropped out. The round still works, but it is *thinner*, and no one can quite remember whose part is missing.

- **Maren Oye.** Bren's older sister, runs the apothecary. Voice: low triangle, dry. "If he hasn't come home by the third bell, we sing him home anyway. That's the rule." Hook: she will pay you in bell-oil if you bring her any of her mother's wax cylinders, which Bren has hidden.
- **Old Ferro.** The oldest person in Hemmel. Voice: a triangle so quiet it is almost gone, with significant master-tape hiss around it. Hook: he claims he remembers the *name* of the dropped-out villager, but only sometimes, and only at certain hours.
- **The Round-Master.** Title, not name; she has forgotten her own name and the village has forgotten too, because that is what the role costs. Voice: the *root* of the round, a sustained low triangle. Hook: she will teach Bren a fragment of the round he has never heard, but only after he admits something true about his mother.

### Stoa (The Bellow)

A trade-town built into the lee of one of the larger stone mouths. The whole town is bowed by the wind on a slow cycle — once an hour, the central square *sings*, low and brassy. Vendors plan their cries around it. Quest hook: the wind has been singing in a different key this season, and the elders are arguing about whether to retune the town or let the wind win.

- **Gellem Stoa-Born.** The mayor, by acclamation, because he has the loudest natural voice. Voice: square-ish, sub-heavy, AR-fast. "We do not retune. We are not a small town." Hook: wants the party to investigate why the wind has changed.
- **Ifa the Reed-Cutter.** A craftswoman who shapes the stone-mouths' interiors. Voice: a high FM tone with a slow flutter. Hook: she will sell you a relic, the **Bowed Reed Pipe** (adds a slow attack-modulation to your weapon's voice), if you bring her three intact reed-stones from the basin.
- **The Broker.** A traveling Tuner, in disguise, gently spreading the gospel of resolution to anyone who has lost someone. Voice: a perfectly-tuned dry sine, no movement. Hook: poses as a grief counselor; players will not realize what he is until later.

### Bellring (Reedmarsh)

The Mage's home academy town. Built on stilts above the marsh, every building is a resonator and the air is so humid that tones sustain unnaturally long. The drone of Bellring is a long, layered, low-passed reed-organ chord. Researchers speak in *intervals* rather than words — a fifth means yes, a tritone means I disagree.

- **Provost Anwen.** Aldine's old mentor. Voice: precise sine, slow envelope, with one cracked partial. Hook: knows what happened to Aldine's hometown. Has not told her. Has been *waiting* for Aldine to ask.
- **Kell the Cylinder-Maker.** Makes wax cylinders for the academy's archive. Voice: dry, granular-ish, with a constant low *click* (his hands are never still). Hook: knows the location of the academy's *forbidden* archive of old recordings. Will not give it up cheaply.
- **The Apprentice Tuner.** A first-year student who has begun, in secret, to practice the Conductor's resolution cadences, because she lost her brother last winter. Voice: triangle, learning to hold itself motionless. Hook: she can be talked out of it. Or not.

### Quay (Glassmouth Coast)

A port-town on the granular sea. The buildings are shaped like seashells and the wind through them produces, all day, the sound of the past few centuries of the town's own conversations, replayed at random. Citizens of Quay have learned not to listen too closely; you can hear yourself saying something you said six months ago, or your grandfather saying something he never told you, or a stranger saying your name. Quest hook: someone in Quay has begun *answering* the granular voices, which is dangerous, because it teaches the granular voices to talk back.

- **Harbormaster Ode.** Runs the docks. Voice: wide pad, slow drift, with grains of dozens of other voices riding it. Hook: needs the party to retrieve a lost cylinder from a wreck offshore.
- **The Singing Child.** A nine-year-old girl who has begun to *duet* with the granular wind. Voice: a clean wavetable scan, layered against the granular pad. Hook: is in real danger of being granulated herself. Lir takes a particular interest.
- **Old Gull.** The town drunk. Voice: detuned saw, frequently out of tune with himself. Hook: the only person in Quay who remembers what it sounded like before the granular pad started. Useful to the Mage.

## 7. Enemies and Bosses

### Enemies

Each enemy is defined by (a) its synthesis voice, (b) its rhythmic behavior on the 16-note grid, and (c) a one-line tactical note.

1. **Chime-Hounds** (Pleach). Pure sine impulses with bell envelopes. Fire on the *downbeats* of every other bar — a slow, predictable pulse. Tactic: the easy tutorial. Players learn ATB by reading the dog's metronome.

2. **Round-Wraiths** (Pleach). Triangle voices that *join the player's own round* — they harmonize with the party's combat rhythm and gain power the longer the party stays in a fixed pattern. Fire on the offbeats only when the party hasn't varied in 4 bars. Tactic: keep the rhythm changing.

3. **Bowmouths** (The Bellow). Bowed metallic FM tones, very slow attack, microtonal. Fire on long sustained notes that *creep* across the bar. Tactic: hit them during their attack ramp, before the tone lands.

4. **Hush-Crickets** (anywhere near The Hush). A sharp tape-hiss burst with no pitched component. Fire in fast unpredictable stutters, three-against-four against the party's grid. Tactic: their bursts mask other enemies' tells; deal with them first.

5. **Reed-Choirs** (Reedmarsh). Five-voice low-passed saw stacks that fight as a single entity but each voice has its own ATB clock. Fire as a *chord progression* across four bars. Tactic: silencing one voice changes the chord — pick the voice that resolves the chord into something less threatening.

6. **Granulines** (Glassmouth Coast). Granular pads that loop fragments of *the party's own previous attacks* back at them. Fire on whatever rhythm the party last used. Tactic: do something the party hasn't done before. They have no defense against novelty.

7. **Tuners** (humanoid; multiple regions). Tuners are people, not monsters; they fight reluctantly. A Tuner's voice is a perfectly-held sine-pad chord, dry and motionless. They fire long sustained "resolution cadences" that grow louder over four bars and, if they complete, *resolve a party member out of the encounter for a turn*. Tactic: interrupt their cadence with rhythmic disruption — don't out-damage them, out-*move* them.

8. **Cylinder-Wights** (Reedmarsh, secret). Old recordings that have escaped the archive. Each one fights as the *voice of a dead person*, looping a fragment of their last song. Fire as ostinatos, locked to the bar. Tactic: the only way to stop them permanently is to *let the loop finish*, which takes a full encounter.

9. **The Wind Itself** (The Bellow, environmental). Not an enemy in the usual sense; the wind through the Bellow is a constant low FM drone that *modulates the party's own filter cutoffs*, dragging everyone's DEF down for a few beats at a time. Tactic: time aggressive moves to gusts.

10. **Hush-Holes** (The Hush, environmental). Patches of true silence on the field. Walking through one *removes a party member from the mix entirely* for two bars. Tactic: route around, or sing them shut by sustaining a note across the gap.

### Bosses

1. **The Round-Master's Shade** (Pleach boss). The first villager who was resolved, returned as a hush-shade. She is the *missing voice of the Hemmel round*. The fight is structurally a five-voice round, with the four party members holding four parts and the Shade holding the fifth. The Shade does not attack; she *sings*, and her singing is so beautiful the party loses HP from the overwhelm. The win condition is to play *with* her until the round resolves into a full chord, then let her go. Bittersweet.

2. **The Stoa-Wind** (Bellow boss). The wind itself, given form. A massive bowed-FM organism with four slow voices, each in a different microtonal interval. The fight is a duel of *long tones*: the boss holds notes for entire phrases, and the party has to interject in the gaps. Right-stick filter mod is critical — the boss responds to the player's wah, dynamically retuning to *avoid* matching it.

3. **The Reed-Choir Conductor** (Reedmarsh boss). A senior Tuner — gentle, apologetic, devastating. She fights as a five-voice low-passed saw stack with a Tuner-style sustained cadence on top. Her gimmick: every time a party member's HP drops below half, her chord *resolves a step closer* to the party's key, making her attacks harder to interrupt. Players have to fight *sloppy* — deliberately stay out of her implied tonality.

4. **The Sea Itself** (Glassmouth Coast boss). The granular ocean, weaponized. Fights as a wide granular pad that grains the party's *own past attacks* — every move you've made in the run is in this fight, somewhere, looped. Win condition: do something *no one in your party has ever done before*, four times in a row. Forces the player to use abilities they've been ignoring.

5. **The Conductor — Una Vesp** (final boss, Coda). A single sustained perfect chord, vast, loving, motionless. She does not "attack" in any conventional sense; she *resolves*, and resolution pulls every party member toward stasis, gradually flattening their envelopes, slowing their ATB, low-passing their voices. The fight is fought against *time and motion*, not HP. Win condition: maintain enough rhythmic, harmonic, and timbral *variation* across all four party members for sixteen bars without any voice flatlining. The boss does not die. She *moves*. The chord becomes a passing tone. The Score keeps playing.

## 8. Tone / Voice Samples

### Opening narrator paragraph

> Aural is a chord that has been held for as long as anyone can remember, and remembering is part of what holds it. Tonight, in a small triangle-wave town called Hemmel, in a soft valley in the country of Pleach, a voice in the chord has dropped out. The neighbors notice without noticing. The round, sung at dusk as it has been sung at dusk for six generations, is thinner tonight than it was last night. There is a gap in it where someone used to be. No one is sure who. The bells in the valley are running a little flat, but the bells always run a little flat in the rain. The rain comes through. The hymn-keeper, a young man named Bren, stands in the doorway of his mother's old house and listens to the silence where the voice should have been, and decides — not in words, but in something closer to a held breath — that he is going to find out where it went.
